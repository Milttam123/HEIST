Hydrogenation-Assisted Graphene Origami Encapsulated Islet Stem Cell Transplantation (HEIST)

Toshiba Exploravision: Matt Lim, Jenny Zhu, and Lauren Eterno

Type 1 Diabetes and Islet Cell Transplantation

What is Type 1 Diabetes?

  • Affecting 1.6 million Americans, type 1 diabetes is a disorder where a patient's own immune system attacks the beta cells, which produce blood regulatory hormones such as insulin.
  • Thus, patients have to depend on an external insulin source through devices such as insulin pumps and pens.

Islet Cell Transplantation

  • Islet Cell Transplantation is an experimental procedure designed to enable the patient to produce their own insulin through infused donor islet cells.
  • As shown in the diagram, the procedure consisted of obtaining a pancreas from a donor, refining the pancreas so only the islets remain, and infusing the islets back into the patient.

HAGO and 2PP

Hydrogenation-Assisted Graphene Origami (HAGO)

  • Hydrogenation-Assisted Graphene Origami (HAGO) is a hydrogen storage mechanism which uses electric fields to fold graphene into origami structures.
  • We plan to apply this mechanism to islet cell transplantation by encapsulating processed islets in order to prevent the immune system from rejecting cells.

Two-Photon Polymerization (2PP)

  • Two-Photon Polymerization(2PP) is a 3D printing technique that uses photochemical reactions to synthesize photosensitive material. 2PP is able to manufacture polymers up to resolutions of 100nm, making it ideal for our biomedical application of the technology.

Consequences

Adverse Consequences

  • Rejection of materials: we currently know that graphene is biocompatible, but future research is needed to develop the photosensitive and bioabsorbable scaffolding material. If the material is not developed, the immune system may reject foreign materials and nullify the transplantation procedure.

Positive Consequences

  • Our procedure will eliminate the need for donors as the stem cells will come from the patient, not external donors.
  • Also, since differential islet cells can reproduce after infusion will reduce the number of encapsulated scaffolds and donor cells necessary for insulin independence.
